Reading Exercise 1 · Comprehension
Exercise 1
[15 marks]
The Last Library
The old lending library on Crane Street had survived two world wars, a fire, and four floods. Mrs Hartley, the librarian of thirty-two years, had watched the neighbourhood transform around her: the bakery become a coffee chain, the cinema become a car park, the post office close entirely. But the library had endured, its oak doors opening each morning at nine precisely, its shelves sagging pleasantly under the weight of accumulated knowledge. On the last Tuesday of March, however, a letter arrived from the council. "Due to budgetary constraints," it read, "Library services at Crane Street will cease on 30 April." Mrs Hartley folded the letter carefully, placed it under her desk, and began planning the resistance.
1(a) What does the phrase "sagging pleasantly" suggest about the library's shelves? Give two ideas. [2]
Think about what physically causes shelves to sag AND what this implies about the library's history/collection.
1(b) How does the writer use contrast to show the changes in the neighbourhood? Refer to specific details. [4]
1(c) What does Mrs Hartley's reaction to the letter reveal about her character? Use evidence from the text. [4]
1(d) Which word best describes the tone of the final sentence?
A
Despairing — Mrs Hartley has given up hope for the library's survival.
B
Resolute — Mrs Hartley is determined to fight back against the closure.
C
Indifferent — Mrs Hartley does not care about the letter's contents.
D
Confused — Mrs Hartley does not understand what has happened.

Summit Fever
At 7,000 metres, rational thought begins to dissolve. Climbers call it "summit fever" — that peculiar madness that compels exhausted, oxygen-deprived mountaineers to push upward when every sensible instinct screams retreat. The mountain does not forgive mistakes at this altitude. Frostbite claims fingers within minutes; cerebral oedema can render a climber unconscious in hours. Yet season after season, the queues grow longer. Expedition leaders speak grimly of "traffic jams" on the death zone routes, of clients whose corporate sponsorship has outpaced their technical skill, of Sherpa guides carrying both the equipment and, increasingly, the responsibility for other people's ambitions. The summit photograph, it seems, has become more valuable than the summit itself.
2(a) What does the writer mean by "rational thought begins to dissolve"? [2]
2(b) Select the option that best explains the writer's use of the phrase "traffic jams on the death zone routes": [2]
A
It compares crowded climbing routes to urban congestion, making a dangerous situation seem absurdly mundane.
B
It shows that the mountain has been urbanised and now has actual roads.
C
It emphasises that expedition leaders are angry at other climbers.
D
It suggests that mountain climbing has become a sport for anyone to try.
2(c) How does the writer criticise modern mountaineering culture? Write about the methods the writer uses. [4]
2(d) What is the effect of the final sentence: "The summit photograph, it seems, has become more valuable than the summit itself"? [4]
Consider the irony, what it implies about motivation, and the word "seems" — does it introduce doubt?

The Hidden Costs of Fast Fashion
Each year, the global fashion industry produces 100 billion garments — approximately 14 items for every person on the planet. The majority of these will be discarded within a year. The environmental toll is staggering: the industry accounts for 10% of global carbon emissions, more than aviation and shipping combined. Cotton farming for a single T-shirt requires 2,700 litres of water. Synthetic fabrics shed microplastics with every wash; these particles have now been detected in Arctic ice, deep ocean sediments, and human blood. Workers in producing countries often receive wages below subsistence level and face dangerous working conditions. In Bangladesh, Cambodia, and Vietnam, garment workers — predominantly young women — routinely work 14-hour shifts for monthly wages that would not cover a week's rent in the countries where their products are sold. Yet consumer demand shows no sign of slowing.
3(a) Using your own words as far as possible, write a summary of the environmental problems caused by the fashion industry. Write 80–100 words. [5]
